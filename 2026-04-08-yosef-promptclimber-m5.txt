⏺ Here's what's happening:

  The loop's job is to take a text section and extract concepts from it. The
  "score" measures how well the extracted concepts match a gold standard.

  Two sets of test inputs:
  - samples/ — the original raw text sections (messy, varied formatting)
  - samples_normalized/ — the same sections, but cleaned up by running them
  through Claude Haiku first to standardize formatting

  What we found:
  - Current prompt scores 0.8344 on original samples
  - Same prompt scores 0.7773 on normalized samples

  This means the prompt has been hill-climbing to handle the quirks and noise in
   the original samples specifically. When you give it cleaner, more consistent
  input, it actually does worse — probably because the few-shot examples and
  instructions are tuned to noisy input patterns that don't appear in the clean
  version.

  The question this was supposed to answer: "Would normalizing the samples first
   make the loop produce better final prompts?"

  The answer based on this: not obviously. Switching to normalized samples would
   reset the score to ~0.77 and the loop would start re-optimizing from there.
  That might produce a more generalizable prompt in the long run, but it's a
  risky reset when you're currently at 0.8344 and climbing.

  TL;DR: The prompt is slightly overfit to the original sample format.
  Normalized samples aren't a free score boost.

❯ beautiful. what would you say is the problem we're trying to solve? what is   
the research question?                                                          

⏺ At the surface level: can a local LLM reliably improve its own knowledge 
  extraction prompt through automated hill-climbing, without human intervention?

  But the deeper question the normalized experiment is probing is:

  ▎ Does the quality of the evaluation signal determine the quality ceiling of 
  the optimized prompt — or does the optimizer find a genuinely better prompt 
  regardless of input noise?

  Concretely: the loop peaked at 0.8345 on original (noisy) samples. The same
  prompt scores 0.7773 on normalized (clean) samples. That gap suggests the
  prompt has partially overfit to quirks in the raw data — it learned to extract
   well from messy input rather than learning to extract well in general.

  So the norm experiment is asking: if you remove the noise from the training 
  signal, does the loop find a more generalizable prompt, and does it plateau 
  higher?

  If the norm loop climbs past 0.8345, the answer is yes — input quality was the
   ceiling, not the model or the prompt structure. If it plateaus lower, the
  original samples were actually more information-rich despite being noisier.

  The broader implication: in self-supervised prompt optimization, the gold 
  standard and the sample quality are as important as the model or the proposer.
   You can have a perfect optimizer and still plateau low if the evaluation
  signal is weak or biased.

  That's the result worth publishing.

❯ good observation. how would you frame your current thesis?                    

⏺ Thesis:

  ▎ The quality ceiling of self-optimizing prompt engineering is bounded not by 
  the optimizer or the model, but by the fidelity of the evaluation signal.

  ---
  Argument structure:

  Premise 1 — Hill-climbing works. A local LLM (Gemma 4 26B) with a capable
  proposer (Haiku) can improve a knowledge extraction prompt from 0.7903 →
  0.8345 over 50 iterations without human intervention. The mechanism is robust.

  Premise 2 — But the ceiling is artificial. The optimized prompt scores 0.057
  lower on clean inputs than on the noisy inputs it trained on. The loop didn't
  learn to extract better — it learned to extract better from these specific 
  samples.

  Hypothesis — If you improve the evaluation signal (normalized samples), the
  loop finds a more generalizable prompt and plateaus higher. The ceiling moves
  with signal quality, not model capability.

  Experiment — loop_arm_g_norm: same model, same proposer, same starting prompt,
   clean inputs. Currently running. Baseline: 0.7676. Prediction: plateaus above
   0.8345 within 50 iterations.

  Broader claim — In self-supervised optimization loops, sample normalization is
   a first-class concern — equivalent in importance to model size or proposer
  quality. Noisy training signal creates a false ceiling that looks like a model
   limitation but is actually a measurement artifact.

  ---
  The norm loop result is the crux. If it beats 0.8345, you have a publishable
  finding.

❯ how would you name your thesis/school?                                        

⏺ Thesis title: The Fidelity Ceiling — self-supervised optimization converges to
   the noise floor of its evaluation signal, not the task ceiling.

  ---
  School: I'd call it "Signal-Grounded Optimization" — a school of thought that
  says:

  ▎ Before asking how to optimize, ask what you're measuring. The evaluation 
  signal is not a passive observer of performance — it actively shapes the 
  ceiling.

  The core provocation: most ML research treats evaluation as solved and focuses
   on the optimizer, the model, the architecture. This school flips that. The 
  measurement apparatus is the first-class variable.

  ---
  Intellectual lineage it sits within:

  - Goodhart's Law ("when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good 
  measure") — but applied inward, to self-optimizing systems
  - Campbell's Law from social science
  - The observer effect in physics — the measurement disturbs the thing being
  measured

❯ What practical use can come out of this to the developers community? If any?  

⏺ Honest answer — some of it is niche, but three things are broadly useful:

  1. Eval-driven prompt development (the pattern)

  Most developers hand-tune prompts by vibes. The loop pattern — write gold
  standards, score automatically, propose mutations, keep improvements — is
  basically TDD for prompts. Any developer with a measurable task (extraction,
  classification, summarization) can set this up. The hill-climbing is dead
  simple and it works. No ML expertise needed. That's publishable as a
  technique.

  2. "Clean your eval set before optimizing" (the finding)

  This is the practical punchline of the fidelity ceiling thesis. If you're
  benchmarking your RAG pipeline, your agent, your extraction system — and your
  test data is noisy — you'll optimize toward noise and hit a false ceiling.
  You'll think "the model can't do better" when actually your measurement is the
   bottleneck. That's actionable advice for every team shipping LLM features.

  3. Cheap model executes, smart model proposes (the architecture)

  Use a 26B local model to do the work, use Haiku/Sonnet to suggest improvements
   to the prompt. The proposer doesn't need to be fast or cheap — it runs once
  per iteration. The executor runs hundreds of times. This multi-model split is
  a practical pattern for anyone trying to reduce API costs while maintaining
  quality.

  ---
  What's NOT broadly useful: the multi-machine coordination, the consortium
  layer, the specific knowledge graph domain. Those are our plumbing.

  What could be a tool: a lightweight open-source prompt-hill-climber — give it
  a prompt, a scoring function, and gold data, it does the rest. Strip out all
  the selfimprove-specific stuff. That's a weekend project and genuinely useful
  to ship.
